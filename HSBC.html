The Story in Plain English

Imagine the world's most global bank. Founded in Hong Kong in 1865 to finance trade between Asia and Europe. Today HSBC operates in 62 countries, serves 40 million customers, and sits at the crossroads of East and West.

For decades that position was HSBC's greatest strength. Then the world changed. The US and China stopped being partners and started being rivals. Suddenly being the bridge between two hostile superpowers is not an advantage — it is a liability.

HSBC is a bridge between two worlds. The problem is both worlds are now trying to blow up the bridge.

Add in a new CEO trying to restructure a 160-year-old institution, rising loan defaults in Hong Kong property, and a dividend that the market is watching closely — and you have a company under serious pressure. But also one trading at a historically cheap valuation.

Why Is HSBC Under Pressure? — The 5 Risks

Key Risks Ranked by Severity

Five major forces are weighing on HSBC simultaneously. The US-China tension is the most dangerous — it threatens the very business model that made HSBC great. Upload your risk chart as hsbc-chart2.png

  1. 01
    US-China Geopolitical Tension (9/10) HSBC earns ~40% of profits from Asia, reports in USD, and answers to Western regulators. Every escalation in US-China tensions forces HSBC to choose sides — an impossible position for a bank built on being neutral.
  2. 02
    Hong Kong Property Exposure (8/10) Hong Kong commercial real estate values fell 30%+ since 2021. HSBC has significant loan exposure to HK property developers. If defaults rise, loan loss provisions will hammer earnings.
  3. 03
    Structural Restructuring Risk (7/10) New CEO Georges Elhedery is spinning off the Western business and doubling down on Asia. Restructuring costs, job cuts, and execution risk are all real. Investors hate uncertainty.
  4. 04
    Interest Rate Sensitivity (6/10) HSBC's net interest margin boomed when rates rose 2022-2024. As rates fall globally, that tailwind becomes a headwind — NIM compression will squeeze profits in 2025-2026.
  5. 05
    Regulatory Pressure (5/10) UK, US and EU regulators are increasingly scrutinising HSBC's China operations. Sanctions compliance costs and regulatory fines are an ongoing drag on the business.

Valuation: Priced for Disaster, Built for Survival

HSBC trades at 7.2x earnings — trading at a discount to intrinsic value of ~42% per DCF analysis. The market is pricing in a worst-case scenario: a China crisis, a Hong Kong property collapse, and regulatory sanctions all hitting at once. We think that is too pessimistic.

My Recommendation — The Analyst's Call

  • You are buying a £154bn bank at a 30% discount to peers. The market has priced in every bad scenario. The reality is likely less bad.
  • 6.8% dividend yield is exceptional. Even if the share price goes nowhere, you collect a near-7% cash return while you wait for the re-rating.
  • The restructuring is actually good news. Spinning off the Western arm and focusing on Asia-Pacific is what HSBC should have done years ago. Georges Elhedery looks like the right person to do it.
  • China risk is real but overstated. HSBC survived the 1997 Asian crisis, the 2008 financial crash and COVID. It will survive US-China tensions too.
  • 25% upside to our 980p target. Plus a 6.8% dividend while you wait. Plus the 4.1% dividend yield while you wait.
